Nuclear fusion combines two light atomic nuclei into a single heavier one, releasing massive energy. This process occurs in plasma, a hot, charged state of matter distinct from solids, liquids, or gases. Fusion powers the sun and stars, requiring temperatures around ten million degrees Celsius to overcome the electrical repulsion between nuclei. The sun's immense gravity creates the necessary conditions by producing extreme pressure.

Einstein's equation (E=mc^2) explains the energy release in fusion: the resulting nucleus has less mass than the original nuclei, and this mass difference converts to energy. If harnessed on Earth, fusion could provide a long-term, abundant energy source without greenhouse gases or long-lived radioactive waste. Unlike current nuclear fission reactors, which split heavy atomic nuclei and produce hazardous radioactive waste, fusion would be a cleaner alternative. This could revolutionize energy production, offering a sustainable solution to meet the world's growing energy demands.

A pickup truck filled with fusion fuel equals the energy of 2 million metric tons of coal or 10 million barrels of oil, showcasing its immense potential. The fuel for fusion, typically isotopes of hydrogen like deuterium and tritium, is abundant and widely available. Achieving practical fusion energy could significantly reduce reliance on fossil fuels and mitigate climate change. The development of efficient fusion reactors remains a scientific and engineering challenge, but the potential benefits make it a critical area of research and investment.
